I've spent quite a bit of time thinking about what to write for my first post, and in the end, I realized there was no better place to start than by sharing a bit about myself and the journey that brought me here.

If you've read my About page, you already know I wasn't born in the United States. My family immigrated here in 2011 when I was eight, for my mom's work. We thought it would be temporary - a few years before going back home. We applied for permanent residency in 2014, but it wasn't something we thought much about; life just moved forward. Before I knew it, I was in high school, and the reality that we might be staying for good was starting to set in. For the first time, decisions about college, careers, and where I wanted to build my future weren't distant thoughts anymore.

For as long as I can remember, I wanted to become a doctor. Medicine felt like the perfect intersection of science, problem-solving, and service. I never really considered another path - even though, looking back, it was a pretty big risk. Our ability to stay in the U.S. depended on my mom's visa being renewed every few years, and most medical schools don't accept international students. At the time, I paid little attention to that.

I ended up at UT Dallas, majoring in Biology, fully committed to pre-med. Since I couldn't legally work, I poured myself into everything else within my control: ER volunteering, neuroscience research, a medical assistant job, leadership roles, a strong GPA. I was fully invested in becoming a physician.

Then, with three semesters left, everything changed.

Our permanent residency timeline had regressed by two years, and I was approaching twenty-one. This meant I could no longer stay as a dependent under my mom's visa. To remain in the country, I had to switch to a student visa, which quietly closed the door on medical school. I didn't process it right away. It took a while to understand that the plan I'd built my entire life around simply wasn't available to me anymore. And it wasn't because I'd failed at it, but because of a number on a government timeline I had no control over. I had never seriously considered a backup, because I never thought I'd need one. For the first time, I had to ask myself what else I could possibly want.

My options were simple: pursue a higher-level degree, or go back to India. I chose the first.

I looked into everything - healthcare management, bioinformatics, pharma. But the more I researched, the more it became clear that interest alone couldn't drive the decision anymore. I also had to think about stability, sponsorship, and not landing in the same position a few years later. Every road eventually led to the same place: AI.

Ironically, growing up, I swore I'd never work in tech. I used to watch my mom sit in front of a computer typing strings of letters and numbers that made no sense to me, and I could never imagine doing the same. But life has a funny way of changing your perspective. I was twenty-one, and I figured if I could dedicate years to preparing for medicine, I could dedicate myself to learning something new. So instead of computer science, I chose a master's in Business Analytics and Artificial Intelligence.

Rebuilding from scratch meant learning technical skills I didn't have and, just as importantly, learning to network in ways I never had to before - mentorship programs, a data visualization club, every career fair I could find around DFW. Making such a drastic pivot gave me an odd kind of confidence: if I could walk away from years of preparation for medicine and start over completely, I could learn anything I put my mind to.

The job market didn't make it easy. Internship competition was brutal, and everyone seemed to be chasing the same roles. Working part-time at the Career Management Center helped; it gave me a look at hiring from the other side of the table.

Eventually, I landed an interview, and a day later, an internship offer: Product Analyst at NCR Atleos.

That role gave me exactly what I needed - data, programming, analytics, and business, all at once. Learning something in a classroom is one thing; applying it to real problems is another entirely. I threw myself in, and it paid off: a full-time offer, an early graduation, and the official start of my career.

Getting the job wasn't the finish line. If anything, it was the start of another chapter.

Today, six months in, I'm setting my sights on something new. As I keep building knowledge in the ATM payments and networks space, I want to bridge business and AI - my long-term goal is to become an AI engineer, through my work and through what I build on my own time.

This space will be a place for me to document what I'm learning, share projects I'm working on, reflect on mistakes and successes, and track my growth as I continue down this path. I have no idea exactly where this journey will take me, but looking back, I've learned that some of the best opportunities come from the plans that don't work out. And the eight-year-old who thought this move was temporary would probably be glad to know it's led somewhere worth writing about.

So here's to the next adventure!
